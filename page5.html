Social Media and Social Engineering

In today's digitally connected world, social media has become an important aspect of our lives, but it also has risks. Social media platforms are a main source of information that can be exploited to commit online crimes such as identity theft, online harassment, and malware, therefore they pose a threat to the safety and privacy of individuals. It is critical to be aware of these dangers and to take precautions.

In this lesson, we will look at the risks involved with social media and social engineering, as well as how you may protect yourself from such threats.

What is Social Engineering?

Social engineering is a form of cyberattack which makes use of human contact to deceive people into disclosing personal information or acting in ways that compromise their security. It is based on the victim's confidence and desire to reveal information, which can be exploited to steal personal or financial information, anything from passwords to banking details.

Here are some of the ways that social engineering can be used on social media:

Scamming:

Emails or communications that appear to be from a genuine source, such as a bank or social networking site, are examples of phishing attempts. Personal information, such as a password or credit card number, will be requested in the message.

Performance:

Cybercriminals may construct false social media profiles that appear to be those of people you know. They may then send you messages or friend requests using these profiles.

Scams:

On social media, cybercriminals may create fake competitions or giveaways. You may be required to give personal information or pay a charge if you enter these contests.

Be cautious about sharing personal information on social media.

Emails or communications that appear to be from a genuine source, such as a bank or social networking site, are examples of phishing attempts. Personal information, such as a password or credit card number, will be requested in the message.

Be sceptical about messages from unknown people.

If you receive a communication from someone you don't know, don't open any attachments or click on any links.

Be cautious when clicking on links in emails or text messages.

If you're unsure whether a link is genuine, hover over it to get the full URL. If the URL does not appear to belong to the website from which it is meant to be, do not click on it.

Keep your software up to date.

Security patches are frequently included in software updates, which can help defend you from cyberattacks.

Create strong passwords and update them on a frequent basis.

Additionally, you should use a unique password for each website or app.

Be mindful of cyberattack warning signs.

If you receive a message that requests personal information or instructs you to click on a link, you should be cautious. If you are unsure whether a communication is authentic, you may always contact the firm or organisation from which it appears.

Phishing

Phishing attacks include the distribution of fake emails claiming to be sent from a legitimate source. The purpose is to deceive the receiver into disclosing sensitive information such usernames, passwords, or banking details.

Spear Phishing

A specific type of phishing directed towards a specific person, organisation, or business.

Whaling

A specific type of Phishing aimed at high-ranking individuals of an organisaton, such as senior executives.

Pretexting

It exploits a victim's emotions by instilling a feeling of urgency, presenting a bargain that is too good to be true, or attempting to evoke pity in order to scam a victim.

Scareware

A type of malware attack that claims it has identified a a virus or another issue on the device and leads the victim to install a harmful software created by the attacker disguised as a legitimate software.

Baiting

An attacker uses bait to deceive the victim into infecting their own system with malware or exposing important information about themselves. One of the most common forms of baiting is when an attacker leaves a storage device infected with malware in the open. When the victim plugs it in their device, the attacker gains access by infecting the device.

To summarise, social media and social engineering pose possible risks to our online safety. We can protect ourselves and stay secure online by being aware of these threats and adopting precautionary actions. Always remember to think twice before you click, secure your personal information, and be wary of cyber attacks.
